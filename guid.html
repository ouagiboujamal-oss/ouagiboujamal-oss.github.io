LESSON 1

Introduction to Medicine

The Foundation of Medical Practice

1.1 What is Medicine?

Medicine is the science and practice of diagnosing, treating, and preventing disease. It combines biological knowledge with clinical skills to maintain and restore human health. The word "medicine" comes from Latin "medicina," meaning the art of healing. Modern medicine is evidence-based, integrating research findings with clinical expertise and patient values.

1.2 Historical Evolution of Medicine

Ancient Medicine (3000 BCE - 500 CE): Early civilizations developed medical systems based on observation and natural remedies. Hippocrates (460-370 BCE) established medicine as a rational discipline. He created the Hippocratic Oath, still relevant today. Galen (129-210 CE) systematized medical knowledge through animal dissections.

Islamic Golden Age (800-1300 CE): Scholars like Avicenna preserved Greek knowledge and made original contributions. His "Canon of Medicine" was a standard text for centuries. Islamic physicians established hospitals and advanced pharmacology.

Renaissance to Modern Era (1400-present): Vesalius revolutionized anatomy. Harvey discovered circulation. Pasteur and Koch established germ theory. The 20th century brought antibiotics, vaccines, imaging, and molecular medicine.

1.3 Major Branches of Medicine

Basic Sciences: Anatomy, Physiology, Biochemistry, Pharmacology, Pathology, Microbiology, Immunology.

Clinical Specialties: Internal Medicine, Pediatrics, Surgery, Obstetrics & Gynecology, Psychiatry, Family Medicine, Emergency Medicine.

Diagnostic Specialties: Radiology, Laboratory Medicine, Pathology, Cardiology, Neurology.

Surgical Subspecialties: General Surgery, Orthopedics, Neurosurgery, Cardiothoracic Surgery, Urology, Ophthalmology.

1.4 The Human Body: 11 Organ Systems

  1. Integumentary: Skin, hair, nails - protection and temperature regulation
  2. Skeletal: 206 bones - support, protection, blood cell production
  3. Muscular: 600+ muscles - movement and heat generation
  4. Nervous: Brain, spinal cord, nerves - control and communication
  5. Endocrine: Glands and hormones - regulation and metabolism
  6. Cardiovascular: Heart and blood vessels - transport
  7. Lymphatic: Nodes and vessels - immunity and fluid balance
  8. Respiratory: Lungs and airways - gas exchange
  9. Digestive: GI tract and organs - nutrient absorption
  10. Urinary: Kidneys and bladder - waste elimination
  11. Reproductive: Ovaries/testes - reproduction

1.5 Medical Terminology

Medical terms combine roots, prefixes, and suffixes:

  • Roots: cardi (heart), hepat (liver), nephr (kidney)
  • Prefixes: hypo- (below), hyper- (above), intra- (within)
  • Suffixes: -itis (inflammation), -ectomy (removal), -ology (study)

1.6 Evidence-Based Medicine

Evidence-based medicine integrates clinical expertise with the best available research. The hierarchy of evidence (strongest to weakest):

  1. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
  2. Randomized controlled trials
  3. Cohort studies
  4. Case-control studies
  5. Case series and reports
  6. Expert opinion

1.7 Medical Ethics: Four Core Principles

  • Autonomy: Respecting patient decisions (informed consent)
  • Beneficence: Acting in patient's best interest
  • Non-maleficence: "First, do no harm"
  • Justice: Fair distribution of healthcare resources
CLINICAL APPLICATION: The clinical approach follows a systematic process: history taking, physical examination, differential diagnosis, diagnostic testing, diagnosis, treatment plan, and follow-up. This step-by-step method ensures thorough patient evaluation.

1.8 Study Methods for Medical Students

  • Active recall: Test yourself frequently
  • Spaced repetition: Review at increasing intervals
  • Clinical correlation: Relate basic science to cases
  • Visual learning: Draw diagrams and flowcharts
  • Group discussion: Explain concepts to peers

Medicine is a lifelong learning journey. Master these foundations before advancing to organ systems.

LESSON 2

The Cardiovascular System

Heart, Blood Vessels, and Circulation

2.1 Overview of the Cardiovascular System

The cardiovascular system is a closed circuit consisting of the heart, blood vessels, and approximately 5-6 liters of blood. Its primary functions include delivering oxygen and nutrients to tissues, removing waste products (carbon dioxide and metabolic wastes), transporting hormones, regulating body temperature, and maintaining homeostasis. The heart beats about 100,000 times per day, pumping approximately 2,000 gallons of blood through 60,000 miles of blood vessels.

2.2 Heart Anatomy

Location and Size: The heart is a muscular organ about the size of a fist, weighing 250-350 grams. It lies in the mediastinum (between the lungs), with two-thirds of its mass to the left of midline. The apex points downward and left, palpable in the 5th intercostal space at the midclavicular line.

Layers of the Heart:

  • Pericardium: A double-walled sac surrounding the heart. The fibrous pericardium prevents overdistension. The serous pericardium has parietal and visceral layers with pericardial fluid (15-50 mL) reducing friction.
  • Myocardium: The thick muscular layer responsible for contraction. It is thickest in the left ventricle (10-15 mm) and thinnest in the atria (2-3 mm).
  • Endocardium: The thin inner lining continuous with blood vessels. It covers valves and chambers.

Chambers:

  • Right atrium: Receives deoxygenated blood from the superior vena cava (head/upper body), inferior vena cava (lower body), and coronary sinus (heart muscle). Contains the sinoatrial (SA) node.
  • Right ventricle: Pumps blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery. Wall thickness 3-5 mm.
  • Left atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from four pulmonary veins.
  • Left ventricle: Pumps blood to the systemic circulation via the aorta. Wall thickness 10-15 mm due to high pressure requirements.

Valves:

  • Atrioventricular valves: Tricuspid (right) and mitral/bicuspid (left) prevent backflow during ventricular systole.
  • Semilunar valves: Pulmonary and aortic valves prevent backflow during ventricular diastole.

2.3 Coronary Circulation

The heart receives blood through coronary arteries that arise from the aorta just above the aortic valve:

  • Left main coronary artery: Divides into left anterior descending (LAD) and left circumflex (LCx). LAD supplies the anterior wall and interventricular septum. LCx supplies the lateral wall.
  • Right coronary artery (RCA): Supplies the right ventricle, inferior wall, and in 90% of people, the atrioventricular (AV) node and sinoatrial (SA) node.

Venous drainage occurs through the coronary sinus and thebesian veins. Coronary artery disease (atherosclerosis) causes angina and myocardial infarction.

CLINICAL CORRELATION - MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION: Acute myocardial infarction (heart attack) occurs when coronary blood flow is obstructed, usually by a thrombus overlying a ruptured atherosclerotic plaque. Symptoms include crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw, diaphoresis, and dyspnea. ECG shows ST elevation (STEMI) or depression (NSTEMI). Cardiac biomarkers (troponin I/T, CK-MB) confirm diagnosis. Treatment includes aspirin, oxygen, nitroglycerin, morphine, and emergent reperfusion (PCI or fibrinolytics).

2.4 Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle consists of systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation). At a heart rate of 75 bpm, each cycle lasts 0.8 seconds.

Phases:

  1. Atrial systole (0.1 sec): Atria contract, forcing blood into ventricles (contributes 20-30% of ventricular filling). The P wave on ECG represents atrial depolarization.
  2. Isovolumetric ventricular contraction (0.05 sec): Ventricles contract with all valves closed. Ventricular pressure rises rapidly. The QRS complex occurs.
  3. Ventricular ejection (0.25 sec): Semilunar valves open. Blood is ejected into aorta and pulmonary artery (stroke volume ~70 mL).
  4. Isovolumetric ventricular relaxation (0.08 sec): Ventricles relax, pressure falls. Semilunar valves close (S2 heart sound).
  5. Ventricular filling (0.32 sec): AV valves open, blood flows passively from atria to ventricles.

Heart sounds: S1 ("lub") from AV valve closure. S2 ("dub") from semilunar valve closure. S3 (ventricular gallop) may indicate heart failure. S4 (atrial gallop) may indicate ventricular hypertrophy. Murmurs indicate turbulent flow from valve stenosis or regurgitation.

2.5 Cardiac Conduction System

  • Sinoatrial (SA) node: Primary pacemaker (60-100 bpm) located in right atrium. Generates impulses spontaneously.
  • Atrioventricular (AV) node: Delays impulse (40-60 bpm) to allow ventricular filling. Located in interatrial septum.
  • Bundle of His: Conducts to ventricles.
  • Purkinje fibers: Rapidly distribute impulse (20-40 bpm).

Electrocardiogram (ECG): Records electrical activity:

  • P wave: Atrial depolarization
  • PR interval (120-200 ms): AV node delay
  • QRS complex (<120 ms): Ventricular depolarization
  • QT interval: Ventricular depolarization and repolarization
  • ST segment: Early ventricular repolarization
  • T wave: Ventricular repolarization

2.6 Cardiac Output and Regulation

Cardiac output (CO) = Heart rate (HR) × Stroke volume (SV). Normal CO is 4-8 L/min. Stroke volume (70 mL/beat) depends on:

  • Preload: Ventricular filling pressure (Frank-Starling law: increased filling increases contraction force)
  • Contractility: Force of contraction (enhanced by sympathetic stimulation, digoxin)
  • Afterload: Resistance against ejection (increased in hypertension)

Heart rate regulated by autonomic nervous system: sympathetic (increases via norepinephrine) and parasympathetic (decreases via vagus nerve and acetylcholine).

2.7 Blood Vessels and Circulation

Arteries: Carry blood away from heart. Thick muscular walls with elastic tissue to withstand high pressure. Vasoconstriction/vasodilation regulates blood flow.

Arterioles: Control blood flow into capillaries. Major site of peripheral resistance.

Capillaries: Site of exchange (oxygen, nutrients, wastes). Single-cell thickness. Types: continuous, fenestrated, sinusoidal.

Venules and Veins: Return blood to heart. Thin walls, larger diameter, contain valves to prevent backflow. Capacitance vessels (hold 60-70% of blood volume).

Systemic circulation: Left ventricle → aorta → arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins → venae cavae → right atrium.

Pulmonary circulation: Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → pulmonary capillaries → pulmonary veins → left atrium.

COMMON CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASES:
  • Hypertension: Sustained BP >130/80 mmHg. Risk factor for MI, stroke, renal failure.
  • Coronary artery disease: Atherosclerosis of coronary arteries. Presents as angina or MI.
  • Heart failure: Inability to pump adequately. Causes dyspnea, edema, fatigue.
  • Arrhythmias: Abnormal heart rhythms (atrial fibrillation most common).
  • Valvular disease: Stenosis (narrowing) or regurgitation (leaking).
  • Peripheral artery disease: Atherosclerosis in leg arteries causing claudication.

2.8 Physical Examination of Cardiovascular System

Inspection: Observe for cyanosis, edema, jugular venous distention, visible pulsations.

Palpation: Palpate carotid, brachial, radial, femoral, popliteal, posterior tibial, dorsalis pedis pulses. Assess for thrills or heaves.

Auscultation: Listen at 4 areas: aortic (right 2nd ICS), pulmonic (left 2nd ICS), tricuspid (left 4th ICS), mitral (left 5th ICS midclavicular). Identify S1, S2, murmurs, rubs, gallops.

Blood pressure measurement: Systolic (Korotkoff phase I) and diastolic (phase V).

2.9 Diagnostic Tests

  • ECG: Detects arrhythmias, ischemia, infarction, chamber enlargement.
  • Echocardiography: Ultrasound for valve function, wall motion, ejection fraction.
  • Stress testing: Exercise or pharmacological stress with ECG or imaging.
  • Cardiac catheterization: Coronary angiography to detect blockages.
  • Cardiac biomarkers: Troponin, CK-MB, BNP.

The cardiovascular system is central to life. Master its anatomy and physiology before studying diseases.

LESSON 3

The Respiratory System

Lungs, Airways, and Gas Exchange

3.1 Overview of the Respiratory System

The respiratory system enables gas exchange between the atmosphere and blood, delivering oxygen to tissues and removing carbon dioxide. It also plays roles in pH balance, phonation, and defense against pathogens. The system includes the upper respiratory tract (nose, pharynx, larynx) and lower respiratory tract (trachea, bronchi, lungs).

3.2 Upper Respiratory Tract

Nose and Nasal Cavity: Air enters through nares, passes through nasal cavity lined with ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium. Functions: filtration (nasal hairs), warming (turbinates), humidification, and olfaction. Paranasal sinuses (frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid) lighten skull and produce mucus.

Pharynx: Common passage for air and food. Divisions: nasopharynx (behind nose, contains adenoids), oropharynx (behind mouth, contains tonsils), laryngopharynx (leads to larynx and esophagus).

Larynx (Voice Box): Cartilaginous structure (thyroid, cricoid, arytenoid) containing vocal cords. Epiglottis prevents aspiration during swallowing. The glottis is the opening between vocal cords.

3.3 Lower Respiratory Tract

Trachea: 10-12 cm long, 2 cm diameter tube with C-shaped cartilage rings preventing collapse. Lined with ciliated epithelium (mucociliary escalator) that traps and moves particles upward. Bifurcates at carina into right and left main bronchi.

Bronchial Tree: Right main bronchus is shorter, wider, more vertical (common site for foreign body aspiration). Bronchi divide into lobar (secondary) bronchi (3 right, 2 left), then segmental (tertiary) bronchi, then bronchioles (no cartilage), then terminal bronchioles, then respiratory bronchioles, then alveolar ducts, then alveolar sacs.

Lungs: Right lung has 3 lobes (upper, middle, lower). Left lung has 2 lobes (upper, lower) and cardiac notch. Each lobe is divided into bronchopulmonary segments (10 right, 8-9 left). Lungs are covered by pleura: visceral (on lung surface) and parietal (lining thoracic cavity) with pleural fluid reducing friction.

3.4 Alveoli and Gas Exchange

Alveoli are tiny air sacs (300-500 million) with total surface area 70-100 m². Alveolar walls contain:

  • Type I pneumocytes: Thin cells for gas exchange (95% of surface area)
  • Type II pneumocytes: Produce surfactant (reduces surface tension, prevents collapse)
  • Alveolar macrophages: Phagocytic defense

Gas exchange occurs across the respiratory membrane (alveolar epithelium, fused basement membrane, capillary endothelium). Oxygen diffuses from alveoli (PO2 100 mmHg) to blood (PO2 40 mmHg). Carbon dioxide diffuses opposite direction (PCO2 45 mmHg in blood to 40 mmHg in alveoli).

3.5 Mechanics of Breathing

Inspiration (active): Diaphragm contracts (flattens), external intercostals contract → thoracic cavity expands → intrapleural pressure becomes more negative → air flows into lungs.

Expiration (normally passive): Diaphragm relaxes, elastic recoil of lungs and chest wall decreases thoracic volume → pressure increases → air flows out. Forced expiration uses internal intercostals and abdominal muscles.

Pressures: Atmospheric pressure = 760 mmHg at sea level. Intra-alveolar pressure varies during breathing. Intrapleural pressure is always negative (756 mmHg) to keep lungs inflated.

3.6 Lung Volumes and Capacities

  • Tidal volume (TV): 500 mL per breath
  • Inspiratory reserve volume (IRV): 3000 mL (additional air inhaled after normal breath)
  • Expiratory reserve volume (ERV): 1200 mL (additional air exhaled after normal breath)
  • Residual volume (RV): 1200 mL (air remaining after maximal exhalation)
  • Inspiratory capacity (IC) = TV + IRV = 3500 mL
  • Functional residual capacity (FRC) = ERV + RV = 2400 mL
  • Vital capacity (VC) = TV + IRV + ERV = 4700 mL
  • Total lung capacity (TLC) = VC + RV = 5900 mL

Minute ventilation = TV × respiratory rate (12-20 bpm) = 6-10 L/min. Dead space (air not participating in exchange) is about 150 mL.

3.7 Gas Transport

Oxygen transport: 98.5% bound to hemoglobin (each Hb binds 4 O2), 1.5% dissolved in plasma. Oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve: affinity increases with increased PO2, decreased PCO2, increased pH, decreased temperature. Shift right (decreased affinity) occurs with exercise (releases O2 to tissues).

Carbon dioxide transport: 70% as bicarbonate (via carbonic anhydrase in RBCs: CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3-), 23% bound to hemoglobin (carbaminohemoglobin), 7% dissolved.

3.8 Regulation of Breathing

Central chemoreceptors: Located in medulla oblongata, respond to changes in CSF pH (indirectly to CO2). Increased CO2 decreases pH, stimulating ventilation.

Peripheral chemoreceptors: Carotid bodies (carotid artery) and aortic bodies (aortic arch) respond to decreased PO2 (<60 mmHg), increased PCO2, decreased pH.

Other receptors: Stretch receptors (Hering-Breuer reflex prevents overinflation), irritant receptors (cause cough), J-receptors (respond to pulmonary edema).

The respiratory center in medulla and pons generates rhythmic breathing. Dorsal respiratory group controls inspiration. Ventral respiratory group controls forced expiration. Pneumotaxic center in pons fine-tunes rate.

CLINICAL CORRELATION - PULMONARY FUNCTION TESTS:
  • Spirometry: Measures FVC (forced vital capacity), FEV1 (volume in 1 second). FEV1/FVC ratio >0.7 normal. Obstructive diseases (asthma, COPD) decrease ratio. Restrictive diseases (fibrosis) decrease FVC with normal ratio.
  • Peak flow: Monitors asthma control.
  • DLCO: Measures diffusion capacity (decreased in emphysema).

3.9 Common Respiratory Diseases

Obstructive diseases: Difficulty exhaling due to airway obstruction:

  • Asthma: Reversible bronchospasm, inflammation, airway hyperresponsiveness. Triggers: allergens, exercise, cold air. Treatment: bronchodilators (albuterol), inhaled corticosteroids.
  • COPD: Chronic bronchitis (productive cough for 3 months/year × 2 years) and emphysema (alveolar destruction). Main cause: smoking. Treatment: bronchodilators, oxygen, smoking cessation.

Restrictive diseases: Difficulty inhaling due to reduced lung compliance:

  • Interstitial lung disease: Pulmonary fibrosis (thickened alveolar walls). Causes: idiopathic, autoimmune, occupational.
  • Neuromuscular disorders: Muscular dystrophy, ALS affect breathing muscles.

Infectious diseases:

  • Pneumonia: Lung parenchyma infection. Community-acquired (Streptococcus pneumoniae most common), hospital-acquired, aspiration. Symptoms: fever, cough, sputum, pleuritic chest pain. Chest x-ray shows consolidation.
  • Tuberculosis: Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Primary infection often latent. Reactivation causes cavitary lesions. Symptoms: chronic cough, hemoptysis, weight loss, night sweats.

Vascular diseases:

  • Pulmonary embolism: Thrombus (usually from leg DVT) occludes pulmonary artery. Presents with sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypoxia, tachycardia. Diagnosis: CT angiography, V/Q scan. Treatment: anticoagulation.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Increased pulmonary artery pressure. Causes: idiopathic, left heart disease, hypoxia. Right heart failure eventually.

Lung cancer: Leading cause of cancer death. Types: small cell (neuroendocrine, metastatic early), non-small cell (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell, large cell). Risk factor: smoking. Symptoms: cough, hemoptysis, weight loss. Diagnosis: CT, biopsy.

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION OF RESPIRATORY SYSTEM:
  • Inspection: Respiratory rate, pattern, use of accessory muscles, cyanosis, clubbing, chest wall deformities.
  • Palpation: Tracheal deviation, tactile fremitus (increased in consolidation, decreased in effusion).
  • Percussion: Resonance (normal), hyperresonance (COPD), dullness (consolidation, effusion).
  • Auscultation: Breath sounds (vesicular, bronchial), adventitious sounds: wheezes (asthma), crackles (pneumonia, fibrosis), rhonchi (bronchitis), pleural friction rub.

3.10 Diagnostic Tests

  • Chest x-ray: PA and lateral views detect infiltrates, masses, effusions, pneumothorax.
  • CT scan: Detailed lung imaging for nodules, interstitial disease.
  • Bronchoscopy: Visualizes airways, allows biopsy, bronchoalveolar lavage.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG): Measures pH, PaO2, PaCO2, HCO3- (assesses oxygenation, ventilation, acid-base).
  • Pulse oximetry: Non-invasive SpO2 monitoring.

The respiratory system is vital for life. Understanding its anatomy and physiology is essential for managing pulmonary diseases.

LESSON 4

The Nervous System

Brain, Spinal Cord, and Neural Control

4.1 Overview of the Nervous System

The nervous system is the body's control and communication network. It detects stimuli, processes information, and coordinates responses. It consists of the central nervous system (CNS: brain and spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system (PNS: cranial nerves, spinal nerves, ganglia). The nervous system contains approximately 86 billion neurons with trillions of connections.

4.2 Neurons and Neuroglia

Neuron structure:

  • Cell body (soma): Contains nucleus and organelles
  • Dendrites: Receive signals from other neurons
  • Axon: Conducts impulses away from cell body (can be >1 meter long)
  • Axon terminals: Release neurotransmitters
  • Myelin sheath: Insulating layer (Schwann cells in PNS, oligodendrocytes in CNS) speeds conduction via saltatory conduction

Types of neurons:

  • Sensory (afferent): Carry signals toward CNS
  • Motor (efferent): Carry signals from CNS to effectors
  • Interneurons: Connect neurons within CNS

Neuroglia (glial cells): Support neurons

  • Astrocytes: Blood-brain barrier, nutrient support, scar formation
  • Oligodendrocytes: Myelinate CNS axons
  • Microglia: Immune defense
  • Ependymal cells: Produce cerebrospinal fluid
  • Schwann cells: Myelinate PNS axons
  • Satellite cells: Support PNS cell bodies

4.3 Action Potential and Synaptic Transmission

Resting membrane potential: -70 mV (inside negative). Maintained by Na+/K+ ATPase (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in) and potassium leak channels.

Action potential phases:

  1. Depolarization: Sodium channels open, Na+ influx (positive feedback). Threshold -55 mV.
  2. Repolarization: Sodium channels inactivate, potassium channels open, K+ efflux.
  3. Hyperpolarization: Potassium channels remain open briefly.
  4. Refractory period: Absolute (no new AP possible) and relative (stronger stimulus needed).

Synaptic transmission: Action potential reaches axon terminal → voltage-gated calcium channels open → Ca2+ influx → neurotransmitter release via exocytosis → neurotransmitters bind postsynaptic receptors → ion channels open → postsynaptic potential (EPSP or IPSP).

Neurotransmitters:

  • Excitatory: Glutamate, acetylcholine (at NMJ), dopamine, norepinephrine
  • Inhibitory: GABA, glycine, serotonin

4.4 Central Nervous System: Brain

Cerebrum: Largest part, divided into hemispheres connected by corpus callosum. Cerebral cortex has gyri (ridges) and sulci (grooves). Lobes:

  • Frontal lobe: Executive function (planning, decision-making), motor cortex (precentral gyrus), Broca's area (speech production). Damage causes personality changes, paralysis.
  • Parietal lobe: Sensory cortex (postcentral gyrus), spatial awareness. Damage causes neglect, agnosia.
  • Temporal lobe: Auditory cortex, memory (hippocampus), language comprehension (Wernicke's area). Damage causes aphasia, memory loss.
  • Occipital lobe: Visual cortex. Damage causes cortical blindness.
  • Insula: Emotion, interoception.

Basal ganglia: Caudate, putamen, globus pallidus. Regulate movement. Damage causes Parkinson's (tremor, rigidity) or Huntington's (chorea).

Thalamus: Relay station for sensory information (except smell).

Hypothalamus: Regulates homeostasis: temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, pituitary gland control.

Limbic system: Hippocampus, amygdala, cingulate gyrus. Emotions and memory.

Cerebellum: Coordinates movement, balance, fine motor control. Damage causes ataxia, intention tremor.

Brainstem:

  • Midbrain: Visual and auditory reflexes, dopamine production
  • Pons: Sleep, respiration, relay between cerebrum and cerebellum
  • Medulla oblongata: Vital functions: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, vomiting, coughing

4.5 Spinal Cord

Extends from foramen magnum to L1-L2 (ends as conus medullaris). Has 31 segments: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal.

Cross-section:

  • Gray matter (butterfly-shaped): Contains cell bodies. Dorsal horn (sensory), ventral horn (motor), lateral horn (autonomic).
  • White matter: Myelinated axons in tracts. Ascending tracts (sensory) and descending tracts (motor).
  • Central canal: Contains CSF.

Major tracts:

  • Dorsal column-medial lemniscus: Fine touch, vibration, proprioception
  • Spinothalamic tract: Pain, temperature, crude touch
  • Corticospinal tract (pyramidal): Voluntary movement
  • Spinocerebellar tracts: Unconscious proprioception

Spinal nerves: Each spinal nerve has dorsal root (sensory) and ventral root (motor). Form plexuses: cervical (C1-C4), brachial (C5-T1), lumbar (L1-L4), sacral (L4-S4).

4.6 Peripheral Nervous System

Cranial nerves (12 pairs):

  1. Olfactory (I) - smell
  2. Optic (II) - vision
  3. Oculomotor (III) - eye movement, pupil constriction
  4. Trochlear (IV) - eye movement (superior oblique)
  5. Trigeminal (V) - facial sensation, mastication
  6. Abducens (VI) - eye movement (lateral rectus)
  7. Facial (VII) - facial movement, taste anterior 2/3 tongue
  8. Vestibulocochlear (VIII) - hearing, balance
  9. Glossopharyngeal (IX) - swallowing, taste posterior 1/3 tongue
  10. Vagus (X) - parasympathetic, larynx, thorax, abdomen
  11. Accessory (XI) - shoulder shrug, head rotation
  12. Hypoglossal (XII) - tongue movement

Autonomic nervous system: Involuntary control

  • Sympathetic: Fight or flight (thoracolumbar outflow). Norepinephrine. Increases HR, BP, bronchodilation, pupil dilation, inhibits digestion.
  • Parasympathetic: Rest and digest (craniosacral outflow). Acetylcholine. Decreases HR, stimulates digestion, constricts pupils.
  • Enteric: Intrinsic control of GI tract.

4.7 Meninges, Ventricles, and CSF

Meninges: Protect brain and spinal cord

  • Dura mater: Outer tough layer
  • Arachnoid mater: Middle web-like layer
  • Pia mater: Inner layer adherent to brain
  • Subarachnoid space: Contains CSF

Ventricles: CSF-filled cavities: lateral ventricles (2), third ventricle, fourth ventricle. Connected by foramina.

Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF): Produced by choroid plexus (150 mL total, replaced 3-4x daily). Functions: buoyancy, protection, waste removal. Hydrocephalus: excessive CSF accumulation.

Blood-brain barrier: Tight junctions between capillary endothelial cells, surrounded by astrocytes. Protects brain from toxins, pathogens. Permeable to lipid-soluble substances (O2, CO2, alcohol).

CLINICAL CORRELATION - NEUROLOGICAL EXAMINATION:
  • Mental status: Alertness, orientation, memory, speech
  • Cranial nerves: Test each nerve function
  • Motor system: Strength, tone, bulk, coordination
  • Sensory system: Light touch, pain, temperature, vibration, proprioception
  • Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes (biceps, triceps, patellar, Achilles), Babinski (plantar response)
  • Coordination and gait: Finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, Romberg test

4.8 Common Neurological Disorders

Cerebrovascular disease (stroke): Brain ischemia (80%) or hemorrhage (20%). Symptoms depend on affected artery. Middle cerebral artery stroke causes contralateral weakness and sensory loss (face/arm > leg), aphasia (dominant hemisphere). Treatment: thrombolysis within 4.5 hours (ischemic), control BP (hemorrhagic).

Seizure disorders: Abnormal neuronal discharges. Generalized tonic-clonic (grand mal): loss of consciousness, convulsions. Absence (petit mal): brief staring spells. Status epilepticus: prolonged seizure >5 minutes (emergency).

Headache: Tension (most common), migraine (throbbing, unilateral, nausea, photophobia), cluster (severe periorbital pain).

Neurodegenerative diseases:

  • Alzheimer's disease: Most common dementia. Memory loss, cognitive decline. Pathology: amyloid plaques, neurofibrillary tangles.
  • Parkinson's disease: Dopamine deficiency. Tremor at rest, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability.
  • Huntington's disease: Genetic (CAG repeat). Chorea, dementia, psychiatric symptoms.
  • Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS): Upper and lower motor neuron degeneration. Progressive weakness, death from respiratory failure.

Demyelinating diseases:

  • Multiple sclerosis: Autoimmune destruction of CNS myelin. Relapsing-remitting course. Symptoms: optic neuritis, weakness, sensory loss, ataxia.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: Post-infectious demyelination of peripheral nerves. Ascending paralysis, areflexia.

Infections:

  • Meningitis: Inflammation of meninges. Bacterial (Neisseria meningitidis, Streptococcus pneumoniae): fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia. Kernig and Brudzinski signs. LP shows elevated WBC, low glucose, high protein.
  • Encephalitis: Brain parenchyma inflammation. Viral (HSV, arboviruses). Altered mental status, seizures, focal deficits.

Spinal cord disorders:

  • Spinal cord compression: From tumor, trauma, disc herniation. Motor, sensory, and autonomic deficits below level.
  • Syringomyelia: Fluid-filled cavity in cord. Causes dissociated sensory loss (loss of pain/temperature with preserved touch).
DIAGNOSTIC TESTS:
  • CT scan: Rapid imaging for trauma, hemorrhage
  • MRI: Detailed brain/spinal cord imaging (best for MS, tumors)
  • EEG: Records brain electrical activity (seizures)
  • EMG/NCS: Nerve and muscle function
  • Lumbar puncture: CSF analysis (infection, inflammation, SAH)

The nervous system is exquisitely complex. Master its organization before approaching clinical neurology.

LESSON 5

The Musculoskeletal System

Bones, Joints, Muscles, and Movement

5.1 Overview of the Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system provides structure, support, and movement. It consists of the skeletal system (206 bones, joints, cartilage) and muscular system (over 600 muscles). This system protects vital organs, produces blood cells, stores minerals, generates heat, and enables locomotion. Understanding its anatomy and physiology is essential for orthopedics, rheumatology, and rehabilitation medicine.

5.2 Bone Structure and Classification

Bone composition: Bones are living organs composed of cells (osteoblasts, osteoclasts, osteocytes) embedded in mineralized matrix (calcium phosphate, collagen). Compact bone forms dense outer layer; spongy (cancellous) bone forms porous inner layer containing marrow.

Bone classification by shape:

  • Long bones: Femur, humerus, tibia, fibula, radius, ulna, phalanges. Longer than wide, with shaft (diaphysis), ends (epiphyses), and growth plates (metaphysis in children).
  • Short bones: Carpals (wrist), tarsals (ankle). Cube-shaped.
  • Flat bones: Skull bones, ribs, sternum, scapulae. Protective, muscle attachment.
  • Irregular bones: Vertebrae, facial bones, pelvic bones. Complex shape.
  • Sesamoid bones: Patella, pisiform. Develop within tendons.

5.3 Bone Development and Remodeling

Ossification: Two types:

  • Intramembranous ossification: Flat bones form directly from mesenchymal tissue (skull, clavicle).
  • Endochondral ossification: Most bones form from hyaline cartilage models. Cartilage is gradually replaced by bone (long bones, vertebrae).

Growth in length: Occurs at epiphyseal (growth) plates. Cartilage proliferates, then ossifies. Plates close after puberty (girls 14-16, boys 16-18).

Remodeling: Bones constantly remodel (about 10% replaced yearly). Osteoblasts build bone; osteoclasts resorb bone. Process regulated by mechanical stress, hormones (PTH, calcitonin, estrogen, growth hormone).

5.4 Joints (Articulations)

Classification by structure and function:

Fibrous joints: No joint cavity, bones held by fibrous tissue, little or no movement:

  • Sutures: Skull bones (immovable)
  • Syndesmoses: Tibia-fibula, radius-ulna (slightly movable)
  • Gomphoses: Tooth in socket (immovable)

Cartilaginous joints: Bones connected by cartilage, limited movement:

  • Synchondroses: Epiphyseal plates (temporary), costochondral joints (immovable)
  • Symphyses: Intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis (slightly movable)

Synovial joints: Most movable, have joint cavity with synovial fluid. Features: articular cartilage, joint capsule, synovial membrane, ligaments. Types:

  • Plane/gliding: Intercarpal, intertarsal, acromioclavicular
  • Hinge: Elbow, knee, ankle (flexion/extension)
  • Pivot: Atlantoaxial (neck), proximal radioulnar (rotation)
  • Condyloid: Wrist (radiocarpal), metacarpophalangeal (flex/extend, abduct/adduct)
  • Saddle: Carpometacarpal of thumb (opposition)
  • Ball-and-socket: Shoulder, hip (multi-axial movement)

5.5 Major Bones of the Body

Axial skeleton (80 bones):

  • Skull (22): Cranial (8) and facial (14) bones, plus auditory ossicles (6) and hyoid
  • Vertebral column (26): 7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, sacrum (5 fused), coccyx (4 fused)
  • Thoracic cage (25): Sternum (3 parts), 12 pairs of ribs (true 1-7, false 8-10, floating 11-12)

Appendicular skeleton (126 bones):

  • Pectoral girdle (4): Clavicles (2) and scapulae (2)
  • Upper limbs (60): Humerus (2), radius (2), ulna (2), carpals (16), metacarpals (10), phalanges (28)
  • Pelvic girdle (2): Hip bones (each ilium, ischium, pubis fused)
  • Lower limbs (60): Femur (2), patella (2), tibia (2), fibula (2), tarsals (14), metatarsals (10), phalanges (28)

5.6 Skeletal Muscle Structure

Organization: Muscle → fascicle → muscle fiber (cell) → myofibril → sarcomere (contractile unit). Connective tissue layers: epimysium (surrounds whole muscle), perimysium (surrounds fascicles), endomysium (surrounds fibers).

Sarcomere: Between Z-discs. Contains thick filaments (myosin) and thin filaments (actin, troponin, tropomyosin). A-band (dark, myosin), I-band (light, actin), H-zone (center A-band, myosin only).

5.7 Sliding Filament Theory

Muscle contraction occurs via cross-bridge cycling:

  1. Action potential reaches neuromuscular junction → acetylcholine release → muscle fiber depolarization
  2. Calcium released from sarcoplasmic reticulum
  3. Calcium binds troponin, moving tropomyosin to expose actin binding sites
  4. Myosin heads bind actin (cross-bridge formation)
  5. Power stroke: myosin pulls actin toward center (requires ATP)
  6. ATP binds myosin, causing detachment
  7. Cycle repeats while calcium present

5.8 Major Skeletal Muscles

Head and neck: Masseter (jaw closing), sternocleidomastoid (neck rotation/flexion), trapezius (shoulder elevation).

Back: Latissimus dorsi (shoulder extension/adduction), erector spinae (back extension), rhomboids (scapular retraction).

Chest: Pectoralis major (shoulder flexion/adduction), pectoralis minor (scapular depression).

Abdomen: Rectus abdominis (trunk flexion), external/internal obliques (trunk rotation), transversus abdominis (compression).

Shoulder: Deltoid (abduction), rotator cuff: supraspinatus (abduction), infraspinatus/teres minor (external rotation), subscapularis (internal rotation).

Arm: Biceps brachii (elbow flexion, supination), triceps brachii (elbow extension), brachialis (elbow flexion).

Forearm: Flexor group (wrist/finger flexion), extensor group (wrist/finger extension).

Hip: Gluteus maximus (hip extension), gluteus medius/minimus (hip abduction), iliopsoas (hip flexion).

Thigh: Quadriceps (knee extension), hamstrings (knee flexion, hip extension), adductors (hip adduction).

Leg: Gastrocnemius (plantar flexion), soleus (plantar flexion), tibialis anterior (dorsiflexion).

5.9 Muscle Fiber Types

  • Type I (slow oxidative): Slow-twitch, fatigue-resistant, aerobic. Postural muscles (soleus).
  • Type IIa (fast oxidative-glycolytic): Fast-twitch, moderately fatigue-resistant. Walking muscles.
  • Type IIx (fast glycolytic): Fast-twitch, easily fatigued, anaerobic. Power movements (gastrocnemius).

5.10 Common Musculoskeletal Disorders

Fractures: Types: greenstick (incomplete), transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted, impacted, pathologic. Signs: pain, swelling, deformity, loss of function, crepitus. Healing stages: hematoma → soft callus → hard callus → remodeling.

Osteoporosis: Decreased bone density, increased fracture risk. Common in postmenopausal women. Risk factors: age, female, low calcium/vitamin D, smoking, corticosteroids. Diagnosis: DEXA scan (T-score < -2.5). Treatment: calcium, vitamin D, bisphosphonates.

Osteoarthritis (OA): Degenerative joint disease, cartilage breakdown. Age-related, affects weight-bearing joints (hips, knees, spine). Symptoms: pain with activity, stiffness after rest, crepitus, bony enlargement. X-ray: joint space narrowing, osteophytes.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA): Autoimmune inflammatory arthritis. Symmetric small joint involvement (hands, wrists). Morning stiffness >30 min, swelling, tenderness. Systemic symptoms. Lab: rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP. Treatment: DMARDs (methotrexate), biologics.

Gout: Monosodium urate crystal deposition. Acute monoarthritis (great toe most common). Intense pain, redness, swelling. Associated with hyperuricemia. Treatment: NSAIDs, colchicine, allopurinol (chronic).

Osteomyelitis: Bone infection (usually bacterial). Hematogenous spread or direct inoculation. Symptoms: fever, pain, erythema. Diagnosis: MRI, bone scan, culture. Treatment: IV antibiotics, debridement.

Muscle strains: Muscle fiber tear. Grade I (mild), II (partial tear), III (complete rupture). Treatment: RICE (rest, ice, compression, elevation), NSAIDs, physical therapy.

Tendonitis: Tendon inflammation. Common sites: rotator cuff (shoulder), Achilles, lateral epicondyle (tennis elbow). Treatment: rest, ice, NSAIDs, physical therapy.

Bursitis: Bursa inflammation. Common sites: prepatellar (knee), olecranon (elbow), subacromial (shoulder). Treatment: rest, ice, NSAIDs, aspiration if infected.

MUSCULOSKELETAL EXAMINATION:
  • Inspection: Alignment, symmetry, swelling, erythema, deformity
  • Palpation: Tenderness, warmth, crepitus, effusion
  • Range of motion: Active (patient moves) and passive (examiner moves)
  • Strength testing: 0-5 scale (0=no contraction, 5=normal)
  • Special tests: Lachman (ACL), McMurray (meniscus), Phalen (carpal tunnel)

5.11 Diagnostic Tests

  • X-ray: Initial imaging for fractures, arthritis
  • CT scan: Detailed bone anatomy, complex fractures
  • MRI: Soft tissue (ligaments, tendons, cartilage, muscles)
  • Ultrasound: Tendons, bursae, joint effusion
  • Bone scan: Metastasis, osteomyelitis, occult fractures
  • DEXA scan: Bone density
  • Arthrocentesis: Joint fluid analysis (septic arthritis, gout)

The musculoskeletal system enables human movement. Master its anatomy before approaching orthopedic pathology.
