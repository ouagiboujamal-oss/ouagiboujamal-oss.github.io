Chapter 1

Introduction to Medicine

The Foundation of Medical Knowledge

1.1 What is Medicine?

Medicine is the science and practice of diagnosing, treating, and preventing disease. It encompasses a wide range of healthcare practices evolved to maintain and restore health. The word "medicine" comes from Latin "medicina," meaning the art of healing. Modern medicine combines scientific knowledge with clinical practice to improve patient outcomes.

1.2 History of Medicine

Ancient Medicine (3000 BCE - 500 CE): Early civilizations like Egypt, Greece, India, and China developed medical systems based on observation and natural remedies. Hippocrates (460-370 BCE), the "Father of Medicine," established ethics and observation-based practice. The Hippocratic Oath remains relevant today.

Medieval Medicine (500-1500 CE): Islamic scholars preserved and expanded Greek knowledge. Avicenna's "Canon of Medicine" (1025 CE) was a standard text for centuries. European medicine stagnated during the Middle Ages but revived during the Renaissance.

Modern Medicine (1500-present): Andreas Vesalius revolutionized anatomy with detailed dissections. William Harvey discovered blood circulation (1628). The 19th century brought germ theory (Pasteur, Koch), anesthesia, and antiseptics. The 20th century saw antibiotics, vaccines, and advanced diagnostics.

📌 Key Takeaway: Medicine has evolved from supernatural beliefs to evidence-based practice, with each era building upon previous knowledge.

1.3 Branches of Medicine

  • Basic Sciences: Anatomy, Physiology, Biochemistry, Pharmacology, Pathology
  • Clinical Specialties: Internal Medicine, Pediatrics, Surgery, Obstetrics & Gynecology, Psychiatry
  • Diagnostic Specialties: Radiology, Laboratory Medicine, Pathology
  • Surgical Specialties: General Surgery, Orthopedics, Neurosurgery, Cardiothoracic Surgery
  • Supporting Fields: Public Health, Preventive Medicine, Rehabilitation, Palliative Care

1.4 The Human Body: An Overview

The human body is organized hierarchically: cells → tissues → organs → systems → organism. There are 11 major organ systems working in harmony:

  1. Integumentary: Skin, hair, nails - protection, temperature regulation
  2. Skeletal: Bones, joints - support, movement, blood cell production
  3. Muscular: Muscles - movement, posture, heat generation
  4. Nervous: Brain, spinal cord, nerves - control, communication
  5. Endocrine: Glands, hormones - regulation, metabolism
  6. Cardiovascular: Heart, blood vessels - transport
  7. Lymphatic: Lymph nodes, vessels - immunity, fluid balance
  8. Respiratory: Lungs, airways - gas exchange
  9. Digestive: Stomach, intestines - nutrient absorption
  10. Urinary: Kidneys, bladder - waste elimination
  11. Reproductive: Ovaries/testes - reproduction

1.5 Medical Terminology

Medical terms are built from word parts:

  • Root words: cardi = heart, hepat = liver, nephr = kidney
  • Prefixes: pre- (before), post- (after), hypo- (below), hyper- (above)
  • Suffixes: -itis (inflammation), -ectomy (removal), -ology (study of)
  • Combining vowels: Often "o" connects roots (cardi+o+logy)
💡 Interesting Fact: The human body contains approximately 37.2 trillion cells, and your DNA could stretch from Earth to Pluto and back (about 10 billion miles).

1.6 Evidence-Based Medicine

Modern medicine relies on evidence-based practice: integrating clinical expertise with the best available research evidence. The hierarchy of evidence includes:

  • Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (strongest)
  • Randomized controlled trials
  • Cohort studies
  • Case-control studies
  • Case series/reports
  • Expert opinion (weakest)

1.7 Medical Ethics

Four core principles guide medical ethics:

  • Autonomy: Respect patient's right to make decisions
  • Beneficence: Act in patient's best interest
  • Non-maleficence: "First, do no harm"
  • Justice: Fair distribution of healthcare resources
⚠️ Important: Medical ethics require informed consent, confidentiality, and truth-telling. Violations can lead to legal consequences and loss of license.

1.8 Study Tips for Medical Students

  • Use active recall - test yourself frequently
  • Spaced repetition - review material at increasing intervals
  • Draw diagrams and mind maps
  • Form study groups for discussion
  • Practice with clinical cases
  • Use mnemonics for memorization
  • Take care of your health - sleep, exercise, nutrition
Chapter 2

Cell Biology & Histology

The Building Blocks of Life

2.1 The Cell: Basic Unit of Life

Cells are the fundamental structural and functional units of all living organisms. The human body contains over 200 different cell types, each specialized for specific functions. Understanding cell biology is essential for comprehending physiology, pathology, and pharmacology.

2.2 Cell Structure and Organelles

Plasma Membrane: The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. It's selectively permeable, regulating substance passage. The fluid mosaic model describes membrane structure with proteins floating in lipid bilayer.

Nucleus: The control center contains DNA organized into chromosomes. The nuclear envelope has pores regulating traffic. Nucleolus produces ribosomes.

Cytoplasm: Cytosol (fluid) plus organelles. Site of many metabolic reactions.

Mitochondria: "Powerhouses" produce ATP through cellular respiration. They have their own DNA (maternal inheritance) and double membrane. Cristae increase surface area for ATP production.

Endoplasmic Reticulum: Rough ER (with ribosomes) synthesizes proteins. Smooth ER synthesizes lipids and detoxifies drugs.

Golgi Apparatus: Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for secretion or delivery.

Lysosomes: Contain digestive enzymes for waste breakdown. Peroxisomes detoxify harmful substances.

Cytoskeleton: Microtubules, microfilaments, and intermediate filaments provide structure and enable movement.

2.3 Cell Transport Mechanisms

  • Passive transport: Diffusion (simple and facilitated), osmosis - no energy required
  • Active transport: Requires ATP - sodium-potassium pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in)
  • Bulk transport: Endocytosis (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated) and exocytosis

2.4 Cell Division

Mitosis: Produces two identical daughter cells for growth and repair. Stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, cytokinesis.

Meiosis: Produces gametes with half genetic material. Two divisions create genetic diversity through crossing over and independent assortment.

2.5 Cell Signaling

Cells communicate through chemical signals:

  • Autocrine: Cell signals itself
  • Paracrine: Signals nearby cells
  • Endocrine: Hormones travel through blood
  • Synaptic: Neurotransmitters across synapses

2.6 Cell Cycle and Regulation

The cell cycle includes interphase (G1, S, G2) and M phase. Checkpoints at G1/S, G2/M, and metaphase ensure proper division. Tumor suppressor genes (p53) and proto-oncogenes regulate cycle. Dysregulation leads to cancer.

2.7 Cell Death

Apoptosis: Programmed cell death - normal physiological process. Characterized by cell shrinkage, nuclear fragmentation, no inflammation.

Necrosis: Pathological cell death from injury. Causes inflammation and tissue damage.

2.8 Stem Cells

Stem cells have self-renewal and differentiation capacity. Types:

  • Totipotent: Can form all cell types (zygote)
  • Pluripotent: Can form all body cells (embryonic stem cells)
  • Multipotent: Limited differentiation (adult stem cells)
  • Induced pluripotent: Reprogrammed adult cells

2.9 Histology: Tissue Types

Epithelial Tissue: Covers surfaces, lines cavities. Classified by cell shape (squamous, cuboidal, columnar) and layers (simple, stratified, pseudostratified). Functions: protection, absorption, secretion, sensation.

Connective Tissue: Supports and connects. Types: loose, dense, adipose, cartilage, bone, blood. Extracellular matrix with fibers (collagen, elastin) and ground substance.

Muscle Tissue: Skeletal (voluntary, striated), cardiac (involuntary, striated), smooth (involuntary, non-striated).

Nervous Tissue: Neurons (conduct impulses) and neuroglia (support).

📌 Clinical Correlation: Understanding cell biology is crucial for oncology (cancer treatment), pharmacology (drug action), and regenerative medicine (stem cell therapy).
Chapter 3

Skeletal System

The Body's Framework

3.1 Overview of the Skeletal System

The adult human skeleton contains 206 bones (newborns have about 270, some fuse during development). The skeletal system provides structural support, protects vital organs, enables movement, produces blood cells (hematopoiesis), and stores minerals (calcium, phosphorus).

3.2 Bone Structure and Composition

Macroscopic structure:

  • Periosteum: Outer fibrous membrane with blood vessels and nerves
  • Compact bone: Dense outer layer organized into osteons (Haversian systems)
  • Spongy (cancellous) bone: Porous inner layer containing red marrow
  • Endosteum: Inner membrane lining medullary cavity
  • Bone marrow: Red marrow (hematopoietic) and yellow marrow (fat)

Microscopic structure:

  • Osteoblasts: Build new bone (bone formation)
  • Osteoclasts: Break down bone (bone resorption)
  • Osteocytes: Mature bone cells maintaining matrix
  • Osteoid: Unmineralized bone matrix (collagen, proteoglycans)
  • Hydroxyapatite: Calcium phosphate crystals providing hardness

3.3 Bone Classification

  • Long bones: Femur, humerus - longer than wide, with shaft (diaphysis) and ends (epiphyses)
  • Short bones: Carpals, tarsals - cube-shaped
  • Flat bones: Skull bones, ribs, sternum - protective, muscle attachment
  • Irregular bones: Vertebrae, facial bones - complex shape
  • Sesamoid bones: Patella - within tendons

3.4 Axial Skeleton (80 bones)

Skull (22 bones):

  • Cranial bones (8): Frontal, parietal (2), temporal (2), occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid - protect brain
  • Facial bones (14): Maxilla (2), mandible, zygomatic (2), nasal (2), lacrimal (2), palatine (2), vomer, inferior nasal conchae (2)
  • Auditory ossicles (6): Malleus, incus, stapes (smallest bones)

Hyoid bone: U-shaped bone in neck, supports tongue

Vertebral column (26 bones):

  • 7 cervical vertebrae (C1-C7) - C1 (atlas) supports head, C2 (axis) allows rotation
  • 12 thoracic vertebrae (T1-T12) - articulate with ribs
  • 5 lumbar vertebrae (L1-L5) - bear most weight
  • Sacrum (5 fused vertebrae)
  • Coccyx (4 fused vertebrae - tailbone)

Thoracic cage (25 bones):

  • Sternum: Manubrium, body, xiphoid process
  • Ribs: 12 pairs - true ribs (1-7, attach directly), false ribs (8-10, attach indirectly), floating ribs (11-12, no anterior attachment)

3.5 Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones)

Pectoral girdle (4): Clavicles (2) and scapulae (2) - attach upper limbs

Upper limbs (60):

  • Humerus (2) - arm bone
  • Radius (2) and ulna (2) - forearm (radius on thumb side)
  • Carpals (16) - wrist bones (8 per hand)
  • Metacarpals (10) - palm bones (5 per hand)
  • Phalanges (28) - finger bones (14 per hand, 3 each finger except thumb has 2)

Pelvic girdle (2): Hip bones (coxal bones) - each formed by fusion of ilium, ischium, pubis

Lower limbs (60):

  • Femur (2) - thigh bone (largest, strongest)
  • Patella (2) - kneecap (sesamoid bone)
  • Tibia (2) and fibula (2) - leg (tibia bears weight)
  • Tarsals (14) - ankle bones (7 per foot, talus and calcaneus largest)
  • Metatarsals (10) - foot bones (5 per foot)
  • Phalanges (28) - toe bones (14 per foot, similar to fingers)

3.6 Joints (Articulations)

Classified by structure and function:

Fibrous joints: No joint cavity, bones held by fibrous tissue

  • Sutures: Skull bones (immovable)
  • Syndesmoses: Tibia-fibula (slightly movable)
  • Gomphoses: Tooth in socket (immovable)

Cartilaginous joints: Bones connected by cartilage

  • Synchondroses: Epiphyseal plates (temporary), rib-sternum (immovable)
  • Symphyses: Intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis (slightly movable)

Synovial joints: Most movable, have joint cavity with synovial fluid

  • Plane/gliding: Intercarpal joints
  • Hinge: Elbow, knee, ankle
  • Pivot: Atlantoaxial joint (neck), radioulnar joint
  • Condyloid: Wrist (radiocarpal)
  • Saddle: Carpometacarpal joint of thumb
  • Ball-and-socket: Shoulder, hip

3.7 Bone Development and Growth

Intramembranous ossification: Flat bones form directly from mesenchymal tissue

Endochondral ossification: Most bones form from hyaline cartilage models

Growth in length: Epiphyseal plates (growth plates) allow lengthening until puberty

Growth in width: Appositional growth from periosteum

3.8 Bone Remodeling and Repair

Bones constantly remodel (about 10% replaced yearly). Remodeling cycle: activation → resorption (osteoclasts) → reversal → formation (osteoblasts) → quiescence.

Fracture healing stages:

  1. Hematoma formation (immediate)
  2. Fibrocartilaginous callus formation (days)
  3. Bony callus formation (weeks)
  4. Bone remodeling (months)

3.9 Common Skeletal Disorders

  • Osteoporosis: Decreased bone density, increased fracture risk (common in postmenopausal women)
  • Osteoarthritis: Degenerative joint disease, cartilage breakdown
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Autoimmune inflammatory joint disease
  • Osteomalacia/Rickets: Soft bones from vitamin D deficiency
  • Paget's disease: Excessive bone breakdown and formation
  • Scoliosis: Lateral curvature of spine
  • Fractures: Types include greenstick, transverse, oblique, comminuted, spiral
💡 Bone Facts: The femur is the longest and strongest bone. The stapes (in ear) is the smallest (2-3 mm). Your bones are 5 times stronger than steel of the same weight. You lose bone mass as you age - about 0.5% yearly after age 40.
Chapter 4

Muscular System

Movement and Power

4.1 Overview of Muscular System

The muscular system contains over 600 muscles, making up about 40% of body weight. Muscles enable movement, maintain posture, generate heat, and facilitate circulation and digestion. There are three types: skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle.

4.2 Skeletal Muscle Structure

Each muscle is an organ containing muscle tissue, connective tissue, nerves, and blood vessels. Organization: muscle → fascicle → muscle fiber (cell) → myofibril → sarcomere (contractile unit). Connective tissue layers: epimysium (outer), perimysium (around fascicles), endomysium (around fibers).

4.3 Sarcomere Structure

The sarcomere is the functional unit between Z-discs. Contains: A-band (dark, myosin), I-band (light, actin), H-zone (center of A-band, myosin only), M-line (center). Actin filaments have troponin and tropomyosin regulatory proteins.

4.4 Sliding Filament Theory

Muscle contraction occurs when myosin heads bind actin, forming cross-bridges and pulling actin toward center. ATP provides energy for detachment and re-cocking. The cycle continues as long as calcium is present.

4.5 Neuromuscular Junction

Motor neuron releases acetylcholine, which binds receptors on muscle fiber, generating action potential. Acetylcholinesterase breaks down ACh to end stimulation.

4.6 Excitation-Contraction Coupling

Action potential travels along sarcolemma and T-tubules, triggering calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum. Calcium binds troponin, moving tropomyosin to expose actin-binding sites. Cross-bridge cycling begins.

4.7 Muscle Fiber Types

  • Type I (slow oxidative): Slow-twitch, fatigue-resistant, aerobic (postural muscles)
  • Type IIa (fast oxidative-glycolytic): Fast-twitch, moderately fatigue-resistant
  • Type IIx (fast glycolytic): Fast-twitch, easily fatigued, anaerobic (power movements)

4.8 Major Skeletal Muscles

Head and neck: Masseter (chewing), sternocleidomastoid (neck rotation), trapezius (shoulder elevation)

Back: Latissimus dorsi (shoulder extension), erector spinae (back extension)

Chest: Pectoralis major (arm flexion/adduction), pectoralis minor

Abdomen: Rectus abdominis (flexion), external/internal obliques (rotation), transversus abdominis (compression)

Shoulder: Deltoid (abduction), rotator cuff (SITS: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis)

Arm: Biceps brachii (flexion), triceps brachii (extension), brachialis

Forearm: Flexor and extensor groups for wrist/fingers

Hip: Gluteus maximus (extension), gluteus medius/minimus (abduction), iliopsoas (flexion)

Thigh: Quadriceps (extension), hamstrings (flexion), adductors (adduction)

Leg: Gastrocnemius (plantar flexion), soleus, tibialis anterior (dorsiflexion)

4.9 Muscle Physiology

Twitch: Single contraction from single stimulus. Latent period, contraction period, relaxation period.

Summation: Increased frequency increases force

Tetanus: Sustained contraction from high-frequency stimulation

Motor unit: Motor neuron plus fibers it innervates. Small units for fine control (eye muscles), large units for power (quadriceps)

4.10 Energy Metabolism

  • ATP-CP system: Immediate energy (10-15 seconds)
  • Glycolysis: Anaerobic, produces lactate (30-40 seconds)
  • Oxidative phosphorylation: Aerobic, sustained energy

4.11 Muscle Disorders

  • Muscular dystrophy: Genetic, progressive weakness (Duchenne most common)
  • Myasthenia gravis: Autoimmune, ACh receptor antibodies, weakness with use
  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread pain, tender points
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Muscle breakdown, can cause kidney failure
  • Cramps: Involuntary painful contractions
📌 Clinical Note: Creatine kinase (CK) levels rise in muscle damage. Electromyography (EMG) tests muscle electrical activity.
